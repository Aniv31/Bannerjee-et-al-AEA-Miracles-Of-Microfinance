# Motivation

We know microfinance has been successful in developing countries as a tool for increased credit access but are the reported miracle effects really true? Has microfinance made women more empowered through this increase in credit access? Do they participate more in business?

To test the "miracles" of microfinance, the authors conduct an RCT in the slums of Hyderabad.

# Identification Strategy

- The decision to give access to Spandana (the microfinance group) here is randomized (area-wise), so we can easily test the treatment effects.
- A total of 260 neighbourhoods were covered.
- To ensure no attrition, only long-term residents were chosen for the program. Eventual migration of loan holders was too low to make any large change in treatment effect or bias the sample.
- The balance tables found that there was no significant difference in characteristics of households in treatment and control areas.
- This meant randomization was implemented correctly, though there was a spillover threat later during the experiment when other microfinance organizations started offering loans too. Their entrance was much later than Spandana's entrance and did not muddy the waters.
- The paper uses ITT, which only ensures the effect of access to microfinance, not the actual treatment effect.

# Results

- Profits of already established businesses increases, though there is no big change in number of new businesses. You would expect a lower interest rate would allow more businesses to come up.
- Consumption on durable goods increases but consumption of temptation goods decreased.
- To ensure that there is no false positives due to the multiple outcomes treated, we create a strict criterion of rejecting the null using the Hochberg-corrected p values.
